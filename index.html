01 — The Origin
It started with an obsession with fish.

Long before Web3, Findurnemo was deep in the world of ichthyology — the scientific study of fish. The beauty of Linnaean taxonomy, the romance of geographic locality data, the moral weight of conservation status: these weren't abstract concepts. They were an obsession.

When the NFT space emerged as a medium for digital art and archival, the idea crystallized: what if you could encode a real fish species — its full scientific classification, its home waters, its conservation status — into a single on-chain token? What if the rarity of the NFT mirrored the rarity of the animal?

CryptoFish was born from that question. Not as a speculative asset, but as a permanent record.

02 — The Research
Every fish took hours before a single pixel was drawn.

Each CryptoFish begins not in an art tool, but in scientific literature. Findurnemo cross-references multiple ichthyological databases — FishBase, the IUCN Red List, regional marine surveys — to confirm every taxonomic detail before classification. Order, family, genus, species, subspecies where applicable. Locality pinpointed to the specific reef system, lagoon, or sea where the species is most characteristically found.

Then comes the art. The pixel work is hand-crafted — not generated, not templated. Each fish's silhouette, coloration, and fin structure is studied from reference photographs and scientific illustrations before being translated into pixel art. The color morphs in the collection reflect real documented color variants of the species, not invented variations.

The conservation status is pulled directly from the most recent IUCN Red List assessment for each species. Critically Endangered fish are rare in the collection because they're rare in the world. That was always the design.

03 — The Philosophy
Preservation-first. Archival by design.

Most NFT collections treat rarity as an arbitrary game mechanic — a certain hat, a certain background color, algorithmically distributed to create scarcity. CryptoFish inverts this. Rarity is inherited from nature itself. A Critically Endangered species produces fewer tokens not because of an algorithm, but because that scarcity is real and demands acknowledgment.

The Linnaean system — developed by Carl Linnaeus in the 18th century and still the global standard for biological classification — provides the structural backbone of the entire collection's trait system. It's not a gimmick. It's a 270-year-old framework for understanding life on Earth, applied to a permanent digital ledger.

The goal is an archive that outlasts any single platform, marketplace, or trend: every fish species immortalized on-chain, with its full scientific identity intact.
